Discoverys Using Power BI

The Headline: A $10M Business with a Concentration Problem At the top level, Tri-Fleet Industries generated $10M in total sales across 99,067 units sold over the observed period (2003–2005). On the surface those are healthy numbers. But the moment you look one level deeper into product, customer, geography, and time, a consistent pattern emerges — the business is heavily concentrated in a small number of drivers, which is both a strength and a significant risk. Product Performance: Classic Cars Are Carrying the Business The product bar chart tells one of the most striking stories on the dashboard. Classic Cars generated $3.9M — nearly double the second-best category, Vintage Cars at $1.9M. After those two, the remaining five categories (Motorcycles at $1.2M, Trucks and Buses at $1.1M, Planes at $1.0M, Ships at $0.7M, and Trains at just $0.2M) form a long tail that collectively barely match Classic Cars alone. This means roughly 39% of total revenue comes from a single product category. For a business with seven product lines, that is a dangerous dependency. If demand for Classic Cars softens — due to supplier issues, market shifts, or competition — the entire revenue base takes a disproportionate hit. Vintage Cars provide a reasonable second pillar, but the remaining five categories urgently need investment or strategic review to either grow them or make a deliberate decision to deprioritise them. The product filter tabs at the top of the dashboard (Classic Cars, Motorcycles, Planes, Ships, Trains, Trucks and Buses, Vintage Cars) indicate the dashboard was designed for drill-down analysis per category — a smart design choice that allows product managers to isolate performance without cluttering the main view. Customer Analysis: Two Customers Dominate the Top Ten The horizontal bar chart ranking the top ten customers by revenue is equally revealing. Euro Shopping leads at $912K, followed by Mini Gifts Distribution at $655K. Together those two customers account for approximately $1.57M — a significant share of the $10M total. The remaining eight customers in the top ten range from $201K (Australian Collectables) down to $154K (Anna's Decorations), all clustered relatively tightly compared to the top two. This gap between Euro Shopping/Mini Gifts and the rest suggests that losing either of those two accounts would create a revenue hole that the next eight customers combined could not quickly fill. From a strategic standpoint, this signals the need for dedicated account management for Euro Shopping and Mini Gifts, alongside a deliberate effort to grow mid-tier customers like Australian Collectables and Muscle Machines closer to that $500K–$600K range to reduce dependency. Geography: North America and Europe Dominate, Emerging Markets Are Untapped The world map bubble chart uses bubble size to represent sales volume by country. The picture is clear — North America has the largest single bubble, shown in dark brown/burnt orange, sitting prominently over the United States. Europe shows a dense cluster of smaller bubbles, indicating sales spread across multiple countries rather than one dominant market. Outside those two regions, the picture is sparse. There are a few small bubbles visible over Asia and one near Australia, but South America and Africa show little to no activity. This geographic concentration mirrors the product and customer story — the business is over-indexed on established, mature markets. The opportunity reading here is straightforward: Asia-Pacific and Latin America represent meaningful white space. Even modest penetration in those markets could diversify revenue geographically and reduce the risk of regional economic downturns wiping out a disproportionate share of income. Sales Trend: A Volatile Journey with a Worrying End The time-series line chart covering January 2003 through early 2005 is perhaps the most strategically important visual on the dashboard, and it tells a story of volatility rather than steady growth. Starting at a relatively low base in early 2003, sales climbed modestly through mid-2003, then spiked sharply to approximately $1.75M–$2M around January 2004 — the single highest point in the entire dataset. This peak is dramatic and demands investigation: was it driven by a large one-off order, a seasonal promotion, a new product launch, or an external market event? Understanding the cause of that spike is critical because if it was replicable, it should become a strategic playbook item. After January 2004, sales dropped back sharply and spent the mid-2004 period hovering around $800K–$900K — roughly half the peak. There was a partial recovery toward the end of 2004 reaching close to $2M again, but the trend then declines steeply into early 2005, finishing at the dataset's lowest point near $600K–$700M. This declining tail into 2005 is a red flag. It suggests the business may not have been able to sustain its peak performance and could be losing momentum. Leadership would need to examine whether this reflects seasonal patterns, customer churn, product demand decline, or broader market conditions. The Overarching Story Tri-Fleet Industries is a $10M business with real strength but fragile foundations. Classic Cars and Vintage Cars are the product engine. Euro Shopping and Mini Gifts are the customer engine. North America and Europe are the geographic engine. Every one of those dominant drivers is a concentration risk. The January 2004 revenue peak shows the business is capable of generating significantly higher volumes — the ceiling is not the problem. The problem is consistency and diversification. The strategic priorities that emerge from this dashboard are clear: investigate and replicate the January 2004 conditions, actively develop the mid-tier customer base, diversify product investment beyond Classic Cars, and build a deliberate market entry plan for Asia-Pacific and Latin America.

Solution Achievement Using Power BI

Tri-Fleet Industries had no unified view of its own business. Sales data existed across products, customers, geographies, and time periods — but it was fragmented. Decision-makers could not answer basic but critical questions: Which products are actually driving revenue? Which customers matter most to the business? Where in the world is the business winning or losing? Is the business growing, declining, or just volatile? Without answers to those questions in one place, leadership was essentially flying blind — making resource, budget, and strategy decisions without a consolidated picture of performance. The dashboard solved that by pulling all four dimensions (product, customer, geography, time) into a single decision-ready view, giving executives the ability to see the full business at a glance and drill into any dimension using the product filter tabs.

Recommendations

1. Reduce Classic Cars dependency With 39% of revenue from one category, Tri-Fleet is exposed. Invest in growing Vintage Cars (already showing promise at $1.9M) and review whether low performers like Trains ($0.2M) are worth continued investment or should be phased out. 2. Protect and grow the top two customers Euro Shopping ($912K) and Mini Gifts Distribution ($655K) need dedicated account management and retention strategies. Simultaneously, the business should set revenue growth targets for mid-tier customers like Australian Collectables and Muscle Machines to close the dependency gap. 3. Investigate the January 2004 revenue spike That peak — nearly $2M in a single period — is the clearest signal of what the business is capable of. Understanding whether it was driven by a campaign, a large order, or seasonal demand and then deliberately replicating those conditions should be a top priority. 4. Address the 2005 revenue decline urgently The downward trend entering 2005 is the most alarming signal in the data. A root cause analysis is needed immediately — whether it is customer churn, product demand softening, or lost market share — before the decline becomes structural. 5. Expand into untapped geographies North America and Europe are mature and saturated. Asia-Pacific and Latin America show minimal bubbles on the map despite being large consumer markets. A phased market entry strategy — starting with Classic Cars and Vintage Cars given their proven demand — could meaningfully diversify revenue and reduce regional risk.
